Part I

The Manifesto

Guiding Question

What makes learning meaningful, and how should that shape the way you live, learn, and engage your field of study?

"Purpose/meaning situates in between work and love. The meaning an individual assigns to each are not fixed; it is instead actively shaped through struggle and growth. It is in the effort to make sense of one's work and connection that meaningful learning takes place."

At the beginning of the semester, I was extremely confused about the topic of the class, Learning and The Meaning of Life. I was confused about what the concept of "learning" has to do with "purpose" and "meaning" in life. I struggled to see the connection. All my life, the learning that I have encountered was in the classroom, studying for exams, completing projects and presentations, and preparing for the future through jobs and internships. On the other hand, the purpose and meaning of my life seemed to stem from outside of the classroom, traveling to see new places, making new connections, pursuing excellence in sports. However, during the learning that occurred throughout the semester, I have gradually made the connection between learning and understanding the meaning/purpose of life. I think what Dr. Jasmine Hawkins-White said during our last lecture really sums it up: "life lessons repeat themselves louder each time if we fail to learn from them." I have begun to understand that life itself is a process of learning: it's a journey to learning about myself, my purpose, my values, and what my life entails through experiences.

This statement is inspired by chapter 10, Happiness Comes from Between, of The Happiness Hypothesis by Jonathan Haidt. In chapter 10, Haidt identified love and work as the foundations of happiness. "One can live magnificently in this world, if one knows how to work and how to love, to work for the person one loves and to love one's work" (Haidt, 229). In this sentence, Haidt is saying that happiness exists in the cross section between building meaningful connections with others and pursuing a purposeful work that makes life feel valuable. In the manifesto statement, I've changed Haidt's idea to: "Purpose situates in between work and connection." For starters, I changed "happiness" to "purpose/meaning." To me, purpose/meaning represent the "why" behind actions, the reason that we wake up early in the morning, the reason that we make sacrifices, and the reason that we feel fulfilled. However, I see happiness as an emotion: laughing at a TikTok, eating good food, hanging out at a park… Although a positive emotion, I feel like happiness doesn't quite equal to purpose/meaning, as one can experience the emotion of happiness while being lost. However, I do think Haidt is referring to purpose in his book.

In Chapter 10, Haidt talks about the importance of love in contributing to happiness. For Haidt, the concept of love is not merely romantic, but is actually the connections that we form with the people around us, our friends, our partners, our environment: "No man, woman, or child is an island. We are ultrasocial creatures, and we can't be happy without having friends and secure attachments to other people" (Haidt, 229). I understand Haidt's argument as how we define our relationships with other people contribute to our sense of purpose. During my junior year of college, one of my family friends, who was also studying in New York City, attempted suicide. I was able to take her to the hospital, where I spent nearly twelve to fourteen hours by her side, talking to her about life and encouraging her not to give up. That day became transformative for me because I was also going through a confusing and difficult period, struggling with my own sense of purpose and feelings of depression. But being with her in that moment and helping her stay alive made me realize how precious life is and how grateful I should be for the ordinary things I often take for granted: being healthy, being alive, and still being able to do what I love. In the end, the experience did not only help her; it also shaped my understanding of myself by teaching me to cherish life more deeply and by showing me that part of my own purpose comes from helping others when they need it most. This is what Haidt is saying: when we form meaningful relationships or perform act of kindness that help others, it in turn defines who we are by showing us what feels fulfilling.

Haidt also emphasized the importance of finding meaning through "work." Haidt mentions that we approach work in one of three ways: job, career, or calling. This is similar to Susan Wolf's idea in her essay Meaning in Life. Wolf defined a meaningful life as "lives of active engagement in projects of worth" (Wolf, 205), the state where a person is gripped, absorbed, involved and energized by endeavors which they deem as significant — in other words, their "purpose." For me personally, this manifests in the form of several life projects, such as basketball and career. In these two fields, I'm actively engaged when performing those activities, actively seek out ways to improve myself, and find the fulfillment of self-realization when I achieve goals set out for myself. Haidt defines this as one's "calling," something that's intrinsically fulfilling, contributing to something larger, marked by frequent experiences of "flow" (Haidt, 235).

However, one common misconception on purpose that we learned in this course is that people are born to explore/pursue certain goals or meanings. This is explored in the movie Soul, where the movie depicts newborn souls carrying a badge consisting of their purpose when entering a new life. However, as the movie Soul depicts, there's not actually a destined purpose for all humans to pursue, but only the world for what it is, and we find out what our purposes are as we go and experience.

I believe purpose/meaning are made clearer through the process of learning, and in particular, learning through struggle. Hence the second sentence of the claim: "The meaning an individual assigns to each are not fixed; it is instead actively shaped through struggle and growth." Haidt explored this in chapter 7 of his book. Haidt introduced the concept of posttraumatic growth where severe stress and crisis can produce lasting positive change. He found that people post-trauma can benefit through discovering their hidden strength, deepened relationships, and shifted priorities (Haidt, 149). He highlights that what's important is sense making after growth: "The ability to make sense of tragedy and then find benefit in it is the key that unlocks posttraumatic growth" (Haidt, 157).

Personally, no matter if it is trying something new for the first time, pursuing what we love, or forming long-lasting relationships, whatever we do, we will inevitably face adversities. In my opinion, growth comes from making sense of these adversities and letting the experience and learning that we gained from these adversities determine what we are good at, what we want to do, and most importantly, who we are as individuals. In my past experiences, my most profound, life-deciding growth all comes from facing and overcoming adversities. Like failing a test, constantly being the worst player in my middle school basketball team, failing the client during my first internship, first break up, being depressed in college… There were countless instances in my life where I had "lost" to adversity, but each time, it made me learn something about myself, redefine how I want to live more clearly, and made me realize who I am and what truly matters to me. Victor Frankl also mentioned this during his experience in the concentration camp: when life is slimmed down to the essentials of surviving, we are only left with the purpose to define our response, dignity, and attitude to which we approach adversities. And it is through learning from adversity that we engage in meaningful learning.

Part III

Application

Part A — Personal Philosophy in Practice

Three principles to carry forward from this course into life.

01
From Haidt — Ch. 10
Purpose lives between, not within

Meaningful living isn't found by looking inward alone. It emerges from the quality of my relationships and the depth of my work — and the space where both intersect. In practice, this means I will stop treating purpose as something to discover in isolation, and start cultivating the conditions for it: showing up for people, committing to work that challenges me, and paying attention to what the overlap feels like.

02
From Haidt — Ch. 7
Struggle is a teacher, not a signal to stop

Posttraumatic growth taught me that adversity isn't something to simply survive — it's where the real learning happens. In practice, this means that when I fail at something — a client, a game, a relationship — I will pause and ask: what is this trying to teach me? What can I make sense of here? The goal is not to romanticize pain, but to refuse to let it go to waste.

03
From Soul (2020) + Wolf
Purpose is not pre-assigned, but found through engagement with life

The movie Soul dismantled the idea that I was born with a fixed purpose waiting to be uncovered. Susan Wolf reinforced this: meaningful life comes from active engagement with projects of worth, not from a predetermined calling. In practice, this means I stop being fixated on what my supposed "calling" is and instead lean into the activities and people that make me feel most alive — trusting that clarity will emerge from that engagement, and through the adversities that I engage in.

Part B — Real Estate Sales
Option 1: A Guide for the Field

A Guide to Humanizing Real Estate Through Learning

I was a Real Estate Salesperson for two and a half years during my time in college. Hence it is the profession I will focus on in this part of the essay. Every aspect of real estate sales is entirely transactional — call scripts, contract handling, showings, closing techniques. More importantly, it is a career that promotes no form of cooperation, as it is a cutthroat industry where every agent is for themselves. And because you get paid by commission only, it feels like everything you do is about money. It treats learning as skill acquisition for money, disregarding the process of developing identity, values, or purpose.

Guide 001

What kind of agent do you want to be for your clients?

What is real estate really about?

Because the only way that real estate agents get paid is through commission by completing deals, when you are in the industry for a while, it feels like we are chasing money instead of helping people. When I was doing real estate, clients became another number that I aimed to get on my bank account, and everything became money-oriented: how many $/hours am I aiming for? Is the commission worth going the extra mile? Am I 100% sure that I will be compensated?

By thinking like this, it is not only a truly dehumanizing experience, but we also negate the fact that our clients are committing to a huge decision: buying or renting a property. Jonathan Haidt mentions the framework of elevation and disgust in Chapter 9 of his book. Elevation being the feeling of doing what's morally good, and disgust being the opposite. At the end of the day, real estate should be about helping people and making a real difference in their lives. So, when we are doing real estate, we should pursue what feels elevating — not only a dollar amount on our bank roll.

Guide 002

What kind of mentor do you want to be?

The case for collaboration in a cutthroat industry

The structure of the real estate industry is one that does not promote collaboration. Hence it is a big reason why 80% of new agents quit within 2 years. There are only so many clients and leads that a company receives, so when money enters your pocket, it probably means that it comes out of someone else's. I've had senior agents tell me that when they started in the 90s, they had to lock their lockers when they left their office to prevent agents from the same brokerage from stealing their clients.

This is against our human nature. Haidt argues that reciprocity is the deep human instinct to return favors, insults, and kindness — and the foundation of social life (Haidt, Ch. 3). We are an ultrasocial species, which means the very fabric of our society is built on collaboration. In real estate, new agents need the guidance of older agents to pass down the know-how, so that we not only become better agents, but the industry becomes more evolved altogether. So — what kind of mentor do you want to be for other agents?

Guide 003

How do you handle adversity in real estate?

Learning through what you cannot control

In real estate, you often face a lot of unplanned adversities: clients backing out of a deal last minute, the rental building unable to deliver, tenant disputes, and more. More often than not, you lose a client due to issues you cannot control. How do we handle adversity for things outside our control? Do we admit defeat, or stay resilient?

Victor Frankl reminds us that how we respond to adversity defines who we are. Haidt also emphasizes the importance of sense-making when we face adversity (Haidt, Ch. 7). Obstacles will always happen. In real estate, it is about gathering enough experience and learning through prior adversity to the point that when we are put in difficult situations, we are well equipped to handle them. Hence, learning through adversity really matters.
